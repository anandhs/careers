## What this job is

Athletic trainers are healthcare professionals who provide medical care to athletes and physically active individuals. They assess, diagnose, and treat sports-related injuries, as well as develop preventive injury programs. Their work often includes collaborating with coaches and doctors to ensure optimal care for athletes.

## Day in the life

A typical day for an athletic trainer involves evaluating injuries, creating rehabilitation programs, and providing immediate care during practices or games. They often have to travel with teams to various events and must keep detailed records of athletes’ health and progress.

## Earning potential

The earning potential for athletic trainers varies by region and experience level. In the U.S., salaries typically range from $50,000 to $75,000 annually, with experienced professionals earning more, especially in specialized settings.

## How to get there

To become an athletic trainer, you typically need a bachelor's degree in athletic training, which includes supervised clinical experience. Certification through the Board of Certification (BOC) is also required, and many trainers pursue state licensure. Additionally, many pursue further education to enhance their expertise.

## Skills that help

Key skills for athletic trainers include strong communication abilities, critical thinking for injury assessment, and hands-on skills in injury treatment techniques. Knowledge of exercise physiology and biomechanics can also be highly beneficial.

## Leading companies

Leading companies in the athletic training field include Athletic Training Inc., HealthSports, and FitWell Clinics, which offer specialized training and rehabilitation services.

## Risks and tradeoffs

Athletic trainers may face physical risks, as they sometimes are on the field during high-contact sports. The job can involve long hours, including weekends and holidays, especially during sports seasons. Emotional stress from managing injured athletes can also be a consideration.

## Next 10 year outlook

The demand for athletic trainers is expected to rise over the next decade, driven by an increasing awareness of the importance of physical fitness and injury prevention, especially in schools and professional sports organizations. The growth of recreational sports programs also contributes to this outlook.

## What to know before choosing it

Before choosing a career as an athletic trainer, consider your passion for sports and healthcare. It's essential to enjoy working closely with athletes of various skill levels and to be prepared for the physical and emotional demands of the role.
