## What this job is

Civil engineers plan, design, and help deliver infrastructure projects.

That can include roads, bridges, water systems, airports, transit systems, buildings, land development, and environmental systems. Some civil engineers focus on design and analysis. Others spend more time in project coordination, permitting, inspections, or construction oversight.

## Day in the life

A normal day can involve both office and site work:

- reviewing plans, models, maps, and reports
- using engineering software to design or analyze systems
- coordinating with contractors, agencies, architects, or survey teams
- visiting project sites
- checking compliance with codes, standards, and permits
- helping solve design or construction problems

The mix depends on the role. Some jobs are heavily design-oriented, while others lean more toward project execution.

## Earning potential

Civil engineering can offer stable middle-to-upper-income earnings with room to grow into project management, specialist, or leadership roles.

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics lists median annual pay for civil engineers at **$99,590**, based on **May 2024** wage data.

Pay can differ based on geography, employer type, technical specialty, licensure, and project scale.

## How to get there

A common path looks like this:

- earn a bachelor's degree in civil engineering or a related field
- complete internships or co-op experience if possible
- start in an entry-level engineering role
- work toward the FE exam and, later, PE licensure if the role requires it

Licensure becomes especially important for engineers who provide services directly to the public or want to sign and seal work.

## Skills that help

Helpful skills include:

- math and engineering analysis
- technical writing
- problem-solving
- project coordination
- communication with nontechnical stakeholders
- attention to standards and detail

Because projects are long and multidisciplinary, organization matters almost as much as technical ability.

## Leading companies

Well-known employers in this field include:

- AECOM
- Jacobs
- WSP

Civil engineers also work for governments, utilities, construction firms, and regional engineering consultancies.

## Risks and tradeoffs

Civil engineering has important tradeoffs:

- projects can move slowly because of funding, regulation, and permitting
- deadlines can tighten sharply during construction phases
- some roles require frequent site visits or field conditions
- public infrastructure spending can affect hiring cycles
- licensure takes time and discipline

The work is often meaningful and tangible, but it can involve long project timelines and a lot of coordination.

## Next 10 year outlook

The next decade looks solid for civil engineering because infrastructure renewal is a long-term need.

Demand should benefit from work on transportation, water systems, resilience, and environmental upgrades. Climate adaptation, aging infrastructure, and population growth in some regions may shape where opportunities expand most. Software tools and modeling will keep improving, but the field still depends on engineering judgment, regulatory understanding, and project delivery skills.

Employment may still fluctuate with public funding cycles, so the outlook is positive but not immune to policy and budget decisions.

## What to know before choosing it

This can be a strong path if you like practical problem-solving and want to work on projects that physically shape communities.

It is a good fit for people who can handle long project timelines, documentation, and coordination across many groups, not just technical calculations.
