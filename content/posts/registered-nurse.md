## What this job is

A registered nurse, often called an RN, provides and coordinates patient care.

RNs work in many settings, including hospitals, clinics, schools, home health, and public health. They monitor patients, give medications, document care, communicate with doctors and families, and help patients understand treatment plans.

## Day in the life

The details depend heavily on the setting, but a typical shift may include:

- checking patient conditions and vital signs
- giving medication or treatments
- updating charts and records
- coordinating with doctors and other clinicians
- explaining care plans to patients and families
- responding to urgent changes in patient condition

In hospital settings, the pace can change very quickly. In outpatient or school settings, schedules may be more predictable.

## Earning potential

Registered nursing can provide stable earnings and multiple advancement paths.

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics lists median annual pay for registered nurses at **$93,600**, based on **May 2024** wage data.

Actual pay can vary by region, specialty, employer, overtime, and shift differentials. Nurses in high-cost areas or specialized roles may earn substantially more.

## How to get there

Common paths into registered nursing include:

- completing an approved nursing program
- earning an associate degree or bachelor's degree in nursing
- passing the NCLEX-RN exam
- meeting state licensing requirements

Many employers increasingly prefer or require a BSN, especially for hospital roles and advancement.

## A course plan through high school and college

In high school, a strong path includes biology, chemistry, anatomy or health science if offered, psychology, and statistics. English and communication classes are also important because nurses explain care plans, document clearly, and work closely with patients and families. If your school offers health occupations or clinical observation programs, those can be especially useful.

In college, the plan usually begins with foundational sciences such as anatomy and physiology, microbiology, chemistry, and nutrition. From there, nursing students move into pharmacology, patient assessment, adult health, maternal-child nursing, mental health nursing, and supervised clinical courses. Public health, child development, and medical ethics are also good additions because they widen your understanding of how care works across settings.

## Skills that help

Helpful skills include:

- patient communication
- attention to detail
- emotional steadiness under pressure
- teamwork
- organization
- physical stamina

Nurses also need to handle documentation well, because clear records are part of safe patient care.

## Leading companies

Well-known employers of registered nurses include:

- HCA Healthcare
- Kaiser Permanente
- Mayo Clinic

Many RNs also work for regional hospital systems, outpatient groups, schools, government agencies, and nonprofit healthcare organizations.

## Risks and tradeoffs

This career carries meaningful tradeoffs:

- long shifts and weekend or holiday work are common
- the work can be physically demanding
- burnout is a real risk in understaffed settings
- exposure to illness, stress, and emotionally difficult situations is part of the job
- state licensure and continuing requirements add ongoing obligations

The meaning in the work is a huge draw for many people, but the workload can be intense.

## Next 10 year outlook

The long-term outlook remains strong because healthcare demand keeps growing and the system continues to need trained nurses.

Over the next decade, aging populations, chronic disease management, and care outside traditional hospital settings should keep demand high. Technology will likely change workflows through better charting tools, remote monitoring, and AI-assisted documentation, but it is unlikely to remove the need for hands-on clinical judgment and patient care.

One likely shift is that nurses may keep taking on broader coordination and patient-education responsibilities as healthcare becomes more distributed across settings.

## What to know before choosing it

This can be a deeply meaningful career if you want direct impact on people's lives.

It is also a career where the emotional and physical load is real. Before choosing it, it helps to be honest about whether you are comfortable with shift schedules, urgent situations, and the human side of illness and recovery.
